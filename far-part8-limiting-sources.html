FAR Part 8: Limiting Sources

When you order from GSA Federal Supply Schedules, you normally need to give multiple schedule holders a fair shot. FAR 8.405-6 tells you when and how you can limit that competition.

The Basics

Limiting Sources on Federal Supply Schedules

The three circumstances, what to document, and who needs to approve.

1 How This Is Different from Part 6

FAR Part 6 covers competition on the open market. FAR Part 8 covers ordering from existing Federal Supply Schedules. They are separate authorities with separate rules. When you place an order against a GSA schedule, you do not need a Part 6 J&A. You follow Part 8 procedures instead. Part 8.405-6 specifically addresses when you can limit the number of schedule contractors you solicit, or restrict an order to one source on a schedule.

Each FAR Part has its own lane for restricting competition, and each lane has its own name for the sole source document:

FAR Part 6: Justification and Approval (J&A). Non-commercial, open market procurements above the SAT.

FAR Part 8: Limited Sources Justification. Orders from Federal Supply Schedules (GSA).

FAR Part 12: Commercial Sole Source Justification. Commercial item buys using simplified procedures.

FAR Part 16: Exception to Fair Opportunity. Task and delivery orders off IDIQ and requirements contracts.

When someone says "limited sources justification," a sharp contracting officer hears "GSA schedule order."

2 Three Circumstances for Limiting Sources

FAR 8.405-6 allows you to limit sources in three situations:

1. An urgent and compelling need exists, and following the normal ordering procedures would result in unacceptable delays. This is the schedule equivalent of urgency. The need must be genuine and unforeseen, not just poor planning.

2. Only one source is capable of providing the supplies or services at the level of quality required because the supplies or services are unique or highly specialized. The key word here is "capable." If only one vendor on the schedule can actually meet your specific technical requirements, you can go directly to them.

3. The order is a logical follow-on to an order already issued under the schedule. If you have an existing order and it makes sense to continue with the same vendor for continuity, you can limit the follow-on. However, you need to justify why switching vendors would be disruptive or costly.

3 Documentation Requirements

For orders exceeding the micro-purchase threshold, you need to document the basis for limiting sources. The documentation must include a description of what you are ordering, the specific basis for limiting competition (which of the three circumstances), market research supporting your decision, and a determination by the ordering activity contracting officer that the order represents the best value.

For orders over the simplified acquisition threshold, the documentation requirements are more substantial. You need a full justification that addresses each of the required elements similar to a Part 6 J&A.

For brand name orders on a schedule, you also need to include the rationale for why the specific brand name item is needed rather than a comparable item available from other schedule contractors.

4 Approval Thresholds

The approval authority depends on the dollar value of the order. For orders up to $800K, the ordering activity contracting officer approves. For orders over $800K to $15M, the competition advocate for the ordering activity approves. For orders exceeding $15M, the head of the ordering activity or designee approves. These thresholds mirror the Part 6 J&A approval levels, but they are separate authorities under Part 8.

5 Posting Requirements

Certain limited-source orders have to be posted publicly. For proposed orders exceeding the simplified acquisition threshold, you need to post the justification on GPE (SAM.gov or equivalent) for 30 days if Only One Source. For urgent requirements, you can proceed and post within 30 days after award. The posting requirement ensures transparency even when you are limiting competition.

6 The AF Limited Sources J&A Template

The Air Force provides a formal template for Limited Sources Justification and Approval (J&A) under FAR Part 8. This template structures your justification around ten major sections plus header fields that capture the essential information any reviewer needs to evaluate your decision to limit competition.

Header Fields: Security Markings, Contracting Activity, PR-ID Number, Program/Project, Program Type, Estimated Contract Cost, and Description of Limitation.

Section I: Contracting Activity identifies the organization placing the order and the responsible contracting officer.

Section II: Nature and/or description of the action being processed tells the reader whether this is a new order, follow-on, modification, or other action type.

Section III: Description of supplies/services required to meet agency needs explains what you are ordering and why your organization needs it. Be specific about technical requirements, operational needs, and timeline.

Section IV: Identification of the justification/rationale for limiting sources and demonstration of the contractor's unique qualifications is where the real evaluation happens. This is not a summary or opinion section. You must articulate precisely why the source is being limited on the schedule. Which of the three FAR 8.405-6 circumstances applies? What makes this contractor uniquely capable? Reference specific technical requirements, certifications, integrations, or operational constraints that only this contractor can meet.

Section V: Determination by the ordering activity Contracting Officer that the order represents the best value consistent with FAR 8.404(d) is unique to Part 8 schedule orders. Unlike Part 6 where best value is a preference, here you must affirmatively determine that limiting to one source produces best value for the government. Address price, quality, delivery, and any other relevant factors.

Section VI: Description of the market research conducted and the results documents what you actually looked for and what you found. List the SINs searched, the vendors evaluated, and why alternatives did not meet the requirement. This is your opportunity to prove you didn't just call one vendor and stop.

Section VII: Other facts supporting the justification covers anything else relevant that did not fit neatly into the earlier sections. Infrastructure compatibility, operational continuity, risk, or organizational factors belong here.

Section VIII: Actions to remove or overcome barriers that led to the restricted consideration requires you to explain what steps could be taken in the future to open this up to competition. Are you documenting interfaces so other vendors can integrate? Will you standardize on different tools? This forward-looking section prevents indefinite source restrictions.

Section IX: Certification by the Contracting Officer requires the CO to certify that the justification is accurate and that the recommended limitation is in the best interest of the government.

Section X: Certification by the technical/requirements personnel confirms the technical accuracy of the statements about unique capability or operational constraints.

Limited Sources Justification and Approval

FAR 8.405-6
Section I: Contracting Activity
Contracting Activity: 633 CONS/LGCB, Joint Base Langley-Eustis, VA
PR Number: FD20260318-0087
Program: Cybersecurity Operations Center Upgrade
Estimated Cost: $92,500.00
Section III: Description of Supplies/Services Required
CyberShield Pro v3.1 Enterprise License (50 seats, 12-month subscription) for continuous cybersecurity monitoring integration with the base Palo Alto Cortex XDR endpoint detection and response platform. Required to maintain USCYBERCOM-directed 15-minute incident response capability per TASKORD 22-0187. Baseline network serves 45 operational squadrons with mission-critical communications infrastructure.
Section IV: Justification/Rationale for Limiting Sources
The base network uses Palo Alto Cortex XDR as its endpoint detection and response platform (Contract FA4800-23-F-0001). CyberShield Pro is the only monitoring tool on GSA Schedule 70 that provides native API integration with Cortex XDR for real-time threat correlation. Alternative monitoring tools on the schedule (SolarWinds, Splunk, Datadog) were evaluated and none offer native Cortex XDR integration. Using a non-integrated tool would require manual threat correlation, increasing incident response time from under 5 minutes to an estimated 45 minutes per event, which does not meet the 15-minute response standard. This circumstance satisfies FAR 8.405-6(a)(2): Only one source is capable of providing the supplies or services at the level of quality required because the supplies or services are unique or highly specialized.
Section V: Best Value Determination
CyberShield Pro's GSA Schedule price of $1,850 per seat ($92,500 total) is consistent with GSA pricing for comparable enterprise security monitoring tools. The native Cortex XDR integration eliminates approximately $35,000 per year in manual correlation labor costs and reduces incident response time by 90%, directly supporting mission readiness and operational efficiency. This limitation represents best value to the government under FAR 8.404(d) through technical superiority and quantified cost avoidance.
Section VI: Market Research
Searched GSA eBuy for all SIN 54151S vendors offering cybersecurity monitoring on 10 Jan 2026. Identified 14 schedule holders. Reviewed technical specifications of the 5 vendors offering comparable monitoring capabilities. Contacted 3 vendors (SolarWinds, ph: 555-0147; Splunk, ph: 555-0198; Datadog, ph: 555-0213) to confirm Cortex XDR integration status. All three confirmed they do not currently support native Cortex XDR API integration. See attached market research report dated 10 Jan 2026.
Section VIII: Actions to Remove Barriers
The Communications Squadron will evaluate open-standard monitoring solutions during the next endpoint detection platform review (scheduled FY28). If a platform with open API standards is selected, the cybersecurity monitoring requirement can be competed among multiple schedule holders, expanding competition and reducing long-term costs.
Contracting Officer Certification: I have determined that this limitation to a single source represents the best value to the government and that the circumstances of this acquisition deem only a single source is reasonably available.
